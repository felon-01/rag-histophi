# The American Revolution
The American Revolution (1775–1783) was a struggle for independence between Great Britain and its thirteen American colonies. Tensions grew over taxation, representation, and British control.

Key events included the Battles of Lexington and Concord, Declaration of Independence, and Yorktown campaign. With French support, the colonists achieved victory.

The revolution established the United States and influenced democratic movements worldwide.

The American Revolution (1765–1783) was a pivotal political upheaval in which the 13 American colonies successfully fought for independence from British rule. Here is a detailed timeline of major events and battles:

1765: Stamp Act sparks colonial protests against taxation without representation.

1770: Boston Massacre intensifies anti-British sentiment after British troops kill colonial protesters.

1773: Boston Tea Party protests British tax on tea by dumping tea into Boston Harbor.

1774: First Continental Congress meets in Philadelphia to coordinate response to British policies.

April 19, 1775: Battles of Lexington and Concord mark the start of armed conflict.

June 17, 1775: Battle of Bunker Hill demonstrates colonial resolve despite British victory.

May 10, 1775: Second Continental Congress convenes; George Washington appointed commander-in-chief.

July 4, 1776: Declaration of Independence is adopted, formally declaring the colonies’ intent to separate.

October 1777: American victory at the Battles of Saratoga convinces France to join as an ally.

Winter 1777–78: Harsh winter at Valley Forge tests Continental Army’s endurance.

1781: Siege of Yorktown culminates in British General Cornwallis’s surrender, effectively ending major combat.

1783: Treaty of Paris signed, officially recognizing American independence and ending the war.

The revolution was marked by key leadership from figures like George Washington, revolutionary writings from Thomas Paine, and critical alliances with France and Spain. It fundamentally transformed political ideas about governance, liberty, and rights, influencing democratic movements worldwide
